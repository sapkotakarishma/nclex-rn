About the NCLEX-RN Exam
What is the NCLEX-RN exam and what is it used for?
The National Council Licensure Examination for Registered Nurses (NCLEX-RN) is a standardized, computer-adaptive exam administered by the National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN). It is required by all U.S. states and territories for licensure as a Registered Nurse (RN). The exam tests the minimum competency needed to practice nursing safely and effectively at the entry level.

Key facts:
  • Administered via Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) — questions adjust to your ability level
  • Minimum 85 questions, maximum 150 questions (Next Generation NCLEX format)
  • Maximum time: 5 hours
  • Pass/fail determined by the NCSBN Passing Standard
What is Next Generation NCLEX (NGN) and how does it differ from the classic format?
The Next Generation NCLEX (NGN), launched in April 2023, introduces new question types designed to test clinical judgment more deeply, not just knowledge recall.

New NGN question types include:
  • Extended Multiple Response (Select All That Apply — weighted scoring)
  • Extended Drag-and-Drop
  • Cloze (Drop-Down)
  • Enhanced Hot Spot (matrix-style)
  • Highlight Text
  • Case Studies (a cluster of 6 questions around one patient scenario)
The NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (NCJMM) underlies all NGN questions, testing six cognitive skills: Recognize Cues, Analyze Cues, Prioritize Hypotheses, Generate Solutions, Take Action, and Evaluate Outcomes.
How many questions will I see on the real NCLEX-RN, and what is a passing score?
Under the NGN format (effective April 2023):
  • Minimum: 85 questions (70 scored + 15 pretest)
  • Maximum: 150 questions
  • Time limit: 5 hours (includes an optional 10-minute break at question 50 and 85)
There is no single numeric passing score — the CAT algorithm determines pass/fail based on whether your demonstrated ability level consistently remains above the NCSBN passing standard by the end of the exam. A result of “Near Passing” means you were close but did not meet the standard.
What Client Needs categories are tested on the NCLEX-RN?
The NCLEX-RN test plan is organized around four major Client Needs categories:
  • Safe and Effective Care Environment (Management of Care; Safety & Infection Control) — ~38%
  • Health Promotion and Maintenance — ~9%
  • Psychosocial Integrity — ~9%
  • Physiological Integrity (Basic Care; Pharmacology; Reduction of Risk; Physiological Adaptation) — ~44%
High-yield areas include: Pharmacology, Prioritization, Delegation, Safety, Maternal/Child, Mental Health, and Medical-Surgical nursing.

What is the difference between Learning Mode and Readiness Assessment?
Learning Mode is designed for active studying:
  • No countdown timer — answer at your own pace
  • Immediately see whether your answer is correct after selecting “Check Answer”
  • Full evidence-based rationale for every question
  • Per-question time tracking (so you can identify where you slow down)
  • AI Insight panel with follow-up Q&A per question

Readiness Assessment simulates the real exam environment:
  • Timed: 5-hour countdown with pause capability — no instant feedback until submission
  • Untimed: No clock, but progress is saved to your browser so you can resume across sessions
  • Final score with pass/fail grade at the end

How is my score calculated? What counts as a pass?
In Readiness Assessment mode, your score is the percentage of questions answered entirely correctly:
  • Single-answer questions: 1 point for a correct selection
  • SATA questions: Must select ALL correct options and NONE of the incorrect ones for full credit
  • Pass threshold: ≥70% is graded as PASS in this tool (the real NCLEX uses a different adaptive algorithm)
In Learning Mode, there is no final grade — focus is on understanding rationale, not scoring.
Does this tool save my progress?
Yes — in Untimed Readiness Assessment mode, your answers and current question position are automatically saved to your browser’s localStorage. You can close the tab and resume from where you left off the next time you open the tool in the same browser.

Note: Clearing browser data, using incognito/private mode, or switching browsers will erase saved progress. Learning Mode and Timed Readiness sessions are not persisted across page reloads.
What does the AI Insight panel do?
The AI Insight panel appears after you check your answer in Learning Mode. It provides:
  • A contextual explanation of the concept being tested, tailored to the question topic
  • Key mnemonics, priority frameworks (ABCs, SATA strategy, delegation rules), and clinical pearls
  • A chat interface where you can ask follow-up questions such as:
    • “Why is option C wrong?”
    • “Give me a mnemonic for this drug.”

The AI chat is built into the tool and does not require an internet connection to an external AI service.
Study Strategy & Tips
What is the best strategy for using this tool to prepare for the NCLEX-RN?
A recommended 3-step approach:
  • Step 1 — Learn: Start with Learning Mode → Study by Topic for your weakest areas. Read every rationale fully, even when you get the answer right.
  • Step 2 — Identify Gaps: Use the AI Insight panel follow-up chat to ask “why” after every question. Understanding the reasoning builds clinical judgment — not just memorization.
  • Step 3 — Simulate: Once comfortable with content, run a Timed Readiness Assessment to practice pacing and exam stamina. Review all missed questions in detail afterward.
Pro tips:
  • For SATA questions, treat each option as an independent True/False statement
  • For priority questions, use ABC (Airway → Breathing → Circulation) and “Acute over Chronic”
  • For pharmacology, learn drug classes before individual agents
  • Aim to understand “why” the wrong answers are wrong — distractors appear almost-correct by design
How should I approach Select All That Apply (SATA) questions?
SATA questions are the most commonly missed question type on the NCLEX. Use this framework:
  • Treat each option independently — ask yourself “Is this statement TRUE or FALSE?” for each option without considering the others
  • Do not anchor on a number — there is no rule about how many options should be correct (could be 2 or 5)
  • Beware of absolutes — options with “always” or “never” are often (not always) incorrect
  • Use evidence-based standards — if an option reflects current clinical guidelines, it is likely correct
  • Eliminate clearly wrong options first, then scrutinize the remaining ones
On the NGN NCLEX, SATA questions use partial credit scoring — you earn points for each correct option selected, minus points for incorrect selections.
How do I tackle prioritization and delegation questions?
Prioritization Framework:
  • ABC — Airway → Breathing → Circulation always comes first
  • Acute/new onset > Chronic/expected
  • Actual problem > Potential problem
  • Physiological > Psychosocial (Maslow’s Hierarchy)
  • Unstable > Stable patient
Delegation Framework (5 Rights):
  • RN only: Initial assessment, care planning, patient teaching, unstable clients, newly admitted clients
  • LPN/LVN: Stable patients, routine medication administration, wound care, reinforcing (not initiating) teaching
  • UAP/CNA: ADLs, vitals on stable clients, ambulation (after initial RN assessment), I&O measurement
Remember: The RN delegates tasks — not accountability.
What are the highest-yield pharmacology topics on the NCLEX-RN?
Pharmacology accounts for approximately 12–18% of the NCLEX-RN. High-yield focus areas:
  • Anticoagulants: Warfarin (diet, INR, antidote: Vit K/FFP/4F-PCC), Heparin (aPTT, HIT, antidote: Protamine), DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban)
  • Cardiac meds: Digoxin (hold if HR <60, toxicity signs, hypokalemia risk), Nitrates (hypotension, avoid with PDE-5 inhibitors)
  • Opioids: Respiratory monitoring, Naloxone (Narcan) for overdose
  • Antibiotics: MRSA (vancomycin — nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity), Aminoglycosides (peak/trough), C. diff (use soap, not hand sanitizer)
  • Psych meds: Lithium (0.6–1.2 mEq/L), MAOIs (tyramine diet), Antipsychotics (EPS — treat with Benadryl/Cogentin)
  • Steroids: Never stop abruptly — risk adrenal crisis; taper required
  • Insulin: Glargine (basal, peakless), Lispro (rapid — mealtime), hypoglycemia assessment before administration
